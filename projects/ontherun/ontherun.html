Introduction

One of the best parts of running outdoors is the nature and scenery. It’s what sets it apart from running on a treadmill. It’s a great way to enjoy the fresh air and get a good workout in.

Now how do outdoor runners plan their run? We did some research and found out that most outdoor runners like to run based on distance. Running a consistent distance makes it easy to track your progress.

However, there currently aren’t many useful tools for runners to plan out their path. For example GoogleMaps is really good at telling you the fastest way to get from point A to point B, but doesn’t offer a way to customize the actual path. Apps like MapMyRun and Strava, though designed for runners, also don’t give users a lot of freedom in creating a custom path.

As a result, a lot of outdoor runners end up running the same route over and over again. They like the consistency in distance to track their progress, but it eventually gets boring. So we have this problem of being able to find new, interesting paths that also meets the runner’s fitness needs.

So, we made On the Run.

On the Run is a tool for outdoor runners to create their own custom running experience. On the Run allows its user to draw different routes and get the most out of their workout by calculating the mileage, estimated time, and predicted calories burned. This app changes how runners can keep track of their progress by providing a way to explore new paths but with consistent distances or times.

The Design Process

In order to achieve the final implementation, we went through multiple cycles of researching, making prototypes, getting feedback, and revising our work. We started off brainstorming a bunch of ideas, narrowing it down to one favorite, interviewing potential users, and designing an interface. Once we got the design down we began implementing an interactive prototype using CodeAnywhere.

Ideation

Our first meeting as a group was to brainstorm ideas that would fit within the scope of our project theme, Authoring. We started off by pitching any idea that came to mind and trying to bounce ideas off of each other. We came up with things like a lesson planner for teachers, a recipe book for home cooks, even a way to create a grocery list for the average supermarket goer. After coming up with about ten ideas, we narrowed it down to three favorites:

  1. Cooking substitutions: An app for home cooks to easily find substitutions for missing ingredients.
  2. Lesson planning: A tool for elementary school teachers to plan their daily lesson plan.
  3. Run planner: An app for outdoor runners to plan their route by sketching out a path on a map.

We then presented our three ideas to the entire class in a quick lightning pitch to get a feel for which idea was the most promising. Based on the feedback from our peers and instructors, it seemed that the most exciting idea was a tie between the baking substitutions and run planning apps.

For both ideas we had people say that they’ve encountered the problem it was trying to fix and that they’d be interested in using the app. A couple concerns noted after our critiques were the usability of the baking app (ie how often would someone be missing an ingredient) and the implementation of the running app (having access to relevant data). Taking this into consideration, we decided to interview prospective users in each user group to narrow it down to one project idea.

Rough Transcriptions of Interviews

Interviewee 1: College student who bakes often, has taken and taught the Baking DeCal, sometimes sells baked goods.

  • Q: Do you bake at home? How often?
    A: I bake at home around once or twice a week
  • Q: Do you use recipes? How do you find them?
    A: I just find recipes online depending on what I want to make. There are some websites that are more trustworthy than others.
  • Q: What difficulties have you confronted when you’re following a recipe?
    A: Sometimes I don’t have the right ingredients or tools to follow the recipe exactly.
  • Q: What do you do when you’re missing an ingredient?
    A: There are some substitutions that I know or can look up if I’m missing certain ingredients, but for the most part I try to keep basic baking ingredients in stock. And if I’m missing a key ingredient from a recipe I’ll decide to make it another day.
  • Q: Are there any difficulties in searching for a substitution?
    A: Not really, it’s pretty easy to find them online. Plus I don’t really like risking a different taste or texture if I substitute an ingredient for a recipe I already know I like, so most of the time if I’m missing something I’ll either run to the store to buy it or just not make it.

Interviewee 2: College student who likes to run for exercise.

  • Q: How often do you run outside?
    A: I try to run at least twice a week, maybe three times if I have time.
  • Q: How do you plan your running route?
    A: I usually run from my apartment down the street until I hit Safeway. I know that’s about a mile away from my apartment so it should be around 2 miles round trip.
  • Q: Do you ever switch up your running route?
    A: Not really, since I know that this route will get me a good run in around twenty minutes. Sometimes it gets boring since I’m running the same path every time and it’s just a straight path, but it’s pretty nice and level.
  • Q: What do you think about planning out running routes using maps?
    A: I used Google Maps to find out the distance between my place and Safeway, but the thing is that it’s hard to plan out routes that aren’t straight or routes that are round trip. Google Maps gives you the fastest way to get to a certain location, which isn’t necessarily the way I want to run. The only way I could customize that would be to add several stops.

Findings
Based on our interviews we found that most bakers don’t really see a need for an app dedicated to substitutions. Most of them find a recipe and make sure they have all the ingredients before they start. If they do happen to be missing an ingredient, they usually just look it up online really quickly, and it’s usually just one ingredient missing. On the other hand, the runners said that they don’t really have a tool to efficiently plan their run and would enjoy being able to access information about their workout.

That being said, we decided to go with our running planner idea and conduct some more interviews to delve deeper into what kind of information a runner would like to know about their running route.

Interview Plan:

  1. Briefly introduce ourselves and our purpose for interview.
  2. Questions for the users:
    - How do you plan your running route?
    - Do you change your route each time? How do you find new routes?
    - How do you plan how long you want to run?
    - What kind of information would you like to know about your run?
    - Do you track your running progress?
  3. Thank the user for their time.

Rough Transcriptions of Interviews

Interviewee 1: Casual outdoor runner

  • Q: Is running your main form of exercise? How often do you run?
    A: I try to run around once or twice a week, it’s not my main form of exercise but I’ve been trying to get more into it.
  • Q: What are some of your running goals?
    A: I want to work on my speed. Usually I run two miles every time and try to keep track of how much time it took me.
  • Q: How do you plan your route? Do you change it up or stick to one?
    A: I usually just run the same route down the street and back, since I know it’ll be two miles. I want to keep the same distance so that I know whether or not I was particularly slow or fast.
  • Q: How do you find a new route?
    A: I try looking around Google Maps.
  • Q: How do you plan how long you want to run? For example based on distance or time?
    A: I go based on distance since I want to keep track of time.
  • Q: What kind of information would you like to know about your path?
    A: Definitely the total distance. Maybe the incline and decline or how long it should take me, how many calories I’ll burn.

Interviewee 2: Regular outdoor runner

  • Q: Is running your main form of exercise? How often do you run?
    A: Running is my main form of exercise. I try to run every day after work.
  • Q: What are some of your running goals?
    A: I’m planning on running the Berkeley Half Marathon this fall, so definitely want to keep up and improve my endurance.
  • Q: How do you plan your route? Do you change it up or stick to one?
    A: I usually go running with friends so there’s probably a set of different routes we like to rotate.
  • Q: Do you know how long these routes are?
    A: Not exactly, maybe around 5 miles.
  • Q: How do you plan how long you want to run? For example based on distance or time?
    A: A little bit of both, we’re not too strict on either. I think as the marathon gets closer I’d want to run some routes that are a little longer, but I don’t care too much on how long it takes me.
  • Q: What kind of information would you like to know about your path?
    A: Probably just the distance, maybe whether or not that path is popular/runner-friendly. Having the steepness would be helpful too if I’m trying to plan out a route that I haven’t been on before.

Findings
Based on these interviews we decided to display certain information about the runner’s chosen path. It seems like besides distance, time, calories burned, and incline/decline is important information to a runner. For our prototype, we’d like to display the incline/decline but probably won’t be able to implement it based on actual data but we can definitely include it in our UI. We also thought that we could offer users a prediction of how much time the route should take and how many calories they would burn if they ran at a certain speed. Since some runners are trying to track their progress, we can also offer a separate page for them to log their running statistics.

Mockups

We were most interested in seeing which type of information display people liked to see. In the mockup we created two variations for when the user finishes drawing their route and would like to see more information about their running path.

In one, we have the information box on the bottom of the screen but the information is more crammed. We also have a full screen information box where the data is bigger and easier to read but you lose view of the map.

Feedback
By asking other designers and users, we came to the conclusion that people like the smaller display box, but wanted the data arranged differently. People suggested the use of icons or a carousel where users can swipe through information. Taking this into consideration, we made a revised screen mockup of the information box.

Implementation

At this point we started implementing our interactive prototype on Codeanywhere. Given the time constraints for this project, we wanted to focus on the drawing experience for the prototype, so we used canvas and added a background image of a map for users to draw on.

We set up a server that serves static files like html, js, and css, and on the front-end side, we used paper.js’s event-handlers. To calculate the total distance we added up the distances of small segments of continuously added points added by a Mousemove event.

Heuristic User Evaluation

We implemented two variations, one where the user can freehand draw their path, and another in which the user can add points and the app will create a straight line connecting each point. We then conducted a user evaluation study in which users can test out both variations. We recorded the time it took for them to draw a specified path using each variation and got some feedback.

User Evaluation Findings
Both users preferred the freehand drawing variant, even though it took a little more time for them to draw the instructed path. Both users liked the freedom that drawing a path had that adding points did not. Both users also had some problems with navigating around the map. We decided to add a move functionality to toggle with the drawing functionality. So if the Move button was highlighted, drawing was turned off, and vice versa.

Final User Evaluation

At our final design showcase, users were able to interact with our final implementation. Participants were approached with the task of creating their own one mile course and checking out how the distance, time and calories change.

Results
For the most part the interaction was pretty simple. One thing we saw some people have difficulties with however was that the app starts off in the moving mode and it wasn’t intuitive that you had to click on the arrows icon to switch to drawing mode.

Given the time limitations of this project and the scope of the class, our final implementation showcased the main functionality of our idea, drawing a custom path and calculating the distance, but there were definitely a lot of places our app could grow and improve in, and we got a lot of great feedback on how we could take things to the next level. The biggest one was integrating GoogleMaps and gathering data from GoogleMaps such as incline and decline. Another great idea we received was being able to connect our app with a heart monitor and using that information to tell users whether they should take it easy or push their limits for that day.

Conclusion

This project taught us a lot about the entire design process of making an app. We had to do a lot of repetitions of designing, prototyping, and evaluating to get to our final implementation, and even after that we still have ideas on how to change our app for the better. One thing this class taught all of us was how difficult application design could be and how there are so many designs we interact with everyday that we take for granted.

Furthermore, this project was really exciting to us because most of us have dealt with the problem of trying to find a running path ourselves. It was also super rewarding when at the design showcase people who’ve dealt with this problem too were really interested in our application.
